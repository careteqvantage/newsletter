CMS Proposes Mandatory Nationwide Joint Replacement Bundle Starting October 2027
Reimbursement & PaymentHigh Priority

On April 10, 2026, CMS published the FY 2027 IPPS proposed rule introducing CJR-X (Comprehensive Care for Joint Replacement Expanded), a mandatory nationwide episode-based payment model for hip, knee, and ankle replacements. Most hospitals paid under IPPS would be required to participate beginning October 1, 2027, with 90-day episodes covering all related post-acute care costs — from the procedure through follow-up visits and physical therapy. Critically, ankle replacement is included for the first time, expanding the model's scope beyond the original CJR program. The prior CJR model generated an estimated $112.7 million in net Medicare savings in performance years 6–7 (2021–2023). CMS will accept public comments through June 9, 2026.

Why It Matters: Michigan orthopedic practices operating in IPPS-paid hospitals will be automatically enrolled in CJR-X unless they already participate in the TEAM model. The mandatory 90-day episode accountability creates direct financial risk tied to post-acute care costs — particularly skilled nursing facility utilization, readmissions, and home health — and places pressure on orthopedic service lines to tightly manage the full care continuum for every joint replacement patient. This is the first mandatory nationwide episode payment model in CMS history and represents a structural shift in how orthopedic volume is monetized.
  • Submit formal comments to CMS by June 9, 2026 — document your hospital's current episode cost profile for LEJR patients, model projected target price exposure for hip, knee, and ankle replacements, and identify specific provisions that affect your patient population or payer mix.
  • Assess whether your hospital's participation in TEAM (if applicable) would provide exemption from CJR-X and run a side-by-side financial scenario comparison of total risk and upside under each model before October 2027 implementation.
  • Convene orthopedic service line and post-acute care leadership to map current 90-day episode cost drivers, renegotiate SNF and home health partnerships, and establish standardized care pathways designed to reduce post-acute utilization without compromising outcomes.
📅 Effective Date: October 1, 2027 (proposed)📅 Compliance Deadline: June 9, 2026 (CMS comment period closes)

CMS 2026 Rule Removes 285 MSK Procedures From Inpatient-Only List; ASC Coverage Expands by 289
Reimbursement & PaymentMedium Priority

Effective January 1, 2026, the finalized CMS OPPS/ASC rule removed 285 procedures — predominantly musculoskeletal — from the Medicare Inpatient-Only (IPO) list and added 289 procedures to the ASC Covered Procedures List. A new Level 7 Musculoskeletal APC was created to accommodate complex outpatient MSK cases previously restricted to inpatient settings. CMS is phasing out the IPO list entirely over a three-year period and extended parity between ASC and Hospital Outpatient Department (HOPD) annual payment updates beginning in 2026. Simultaneously, the rule advances site-neutral payment policy by reducing the long-standing payment differential between hospital outpatient departments and physician offices — a shift Congress is actively considering expanding through legislative action.

Why It Matters: For Michigan orthopedic and MSK ASCs, this is the largest single expansion of ASC-eligible procedure codes in recent memory. Practices that have not yet updated case selection criteria, credentialing, or billing workflows to capture newly eligible complex joint and spine cases may be leaving significant volume and revenue on the table. Site-neutral payment convergence also diminishes hospital-affiliated practices' traditional reimbursement advantage, accelerating the competitive case for ASC-based care delivery.
  • Pull the complete list of 289 newly ASC-eligible procedure codes and cross-reference against your current case volume to identify procedures now eligible for ASC-rate reimbursement that are still being routed to hospital outpatient settings.
  • Coordinate with ASC compliance, billing, and coding teams to update charge masters, payer contracts, and pre-authorization workflows to reflect the newly eligible procedure categories under the Level 7 Musculoskeletal APC.
  • Evaluate OR block time, implant vendor contracts, and anesthesia coverage capacity to scale up complex MSK procedures in your ASC as new cases migrate from inpatient settings over the remainder of 2026.

Tibial Osteotomy Does Not Improve Graft Survivorship in Patellofemoral OCA Transplantation
Orthopedic Market TrendsLow Priority

A retrospective study of 103 patients who underwent patellofemoral osteochondral allograft (OCA) transplantation between 2001 and 2023 found that concomitant tibial tubercle osteotomy did not improve graft survivorship among patients undergoing the procedure. The findings, presented in New Orleans and published in Healio Orthopedics on April 17, 2026, challenge the assumption that adding a tibial tubercle osteotomy to patellofemoral OCA transplantation improves long-term outcomes — suggesting that simpler, less invasive surgical approaches may achieve equivalent graft survivorship without the added complexity, recovery burden, and operative time of concomitant osteotomy.

Why It Matters: Michigan MSK and sports medicine surgeons performing cartilage restoration procedures can use this evidence to refine patient selection criteria and surgical planning for patellofemoral OCA cases — potentially simplifying procedures, reducing operative time and complications, and lowering episode cost without compromising outcomes, which is increasingly relevant under episode-based payment models.
  • Review your current surgical protocol for patellofemoral OCA transplantation and assess whether concomitant tibial tubercle osteotomy is being performed routinely versus selectively based on individual patient anatomy and biomechanics — and whether the evidence supports a more selective approach.
  • Present this study at your next MSK or sports medicine case conference and update clinical decision-making criteria for patellofemoral OCA case selection based on the 103-patient retrospective dataset.
  • Incorporate this finding into patient consent conversations for complex cartilage restoration procedures to set appropriate outcome expectations and provide evidence-based surgical rationale for technique selection.
