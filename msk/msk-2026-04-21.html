ASC Intelligence 🔴 HIGH
Congress Introduces Bipartisan Bill to Permanently Stabilize ASC Medicare Reimbursements
Reps. Beth Van Duyne (R-TX) and John Larson (D-CT) introduced the Outpatient Surgery Access Act of 2026 on March 25, 2026, which would permanently align Medicare ASC payment updates with hospital outpatient department rates using the hospital market basket and eliminate ASC-specific budget neutrality adjustments that suppress payments. The Ambulatory Surgery Center Association (ASCA) is the bill's lead industry backer, warning that the current HOPD-parity update policy is set to expire at the end of 2026 — and would revert ASC payment growth to a less favorable inflation index without congressional action.
Why It Matters for Michigan MSK Without permanent legislation, Michigan orthopedic and spine ASCs face a steeper margin decline after 2026 — precisely as payer pressures and supply costs intensify. Engaging congressional representatives now, before the legislative window closes, is the most cost-effective near-term advocacy action for ASC operators.
  • Contact Michigan's congressional delegation to advocate for the Outpatient Surgery Access Act, highlighting your ASC's role in delivering lower-cost orthopedic care and the margin risk of a rate reversion.
  • Model the post-2026 financial impact of a reimbursement rate reversion to quantify margin exposure and support payer negotiation targets if the bill does not advance.
  • Register for ASCA advocacy updates and participate in fly-in campaigns or comment letter drives when activated to increase legislative momentum.

DOJ Sues NewYork-Presbyterian for Using Payer Contracts to Block Patient Steering to ASCs
On March 26, 2026, the U.S. Department of Justice filed a civil antitrust lawsuit against NewYork-Presbyterian Hospital, alleging the system used "all-or-nothing" payer contracts to force insurers to include all NYP facilities in nearly every network — effectively preventing health plans from offering lower-cost benefit designs that would direct patients to ambulatory surgery centers. The DOJ filing explicitly identified ASCs as appropriate, cost-effective alternatives that patients should be able to access through their insurers.
Why It Matters for Michigan MSK A favorable ruling in this case would create a powerful legal precedent that could embolden Michigan payers to build ASC-steerage benefit designs for commercially insured patients — presenting an orthopedic volume opportunity for independent and physician-owned ASCs positioned to accept that traffic.
  • Monitor case proceedings in the SDNY and prepare payer-relations talking points that document your ASC's cost-per-episode and quality outcomes compared to local hospital outpatient departments.
  • Audit current Michigan payer network agreements for "all-or-nothing" or anti-steering language that may conflict with emerging DOJ enforcement expectations and flag clauses for renegotiation.
  • Consult healthcare legal counsel to identify any Michigan-specific payer contract terms limiting patient access to your ASC and map those to upcoming contract renewal windows.

Technology & Innovation 🟡 MEDIUM
World's First ASC Deploys Stryker's Handheld Mako RPS Robot for Total Knee Arthroplasty
Surgery Center at Pelham in Greer, South Carolina became the first ASC in the world to perform a total knee arthroplasty using Stryker's Mako Robotic Power System (RPS). The handheld device is engineered specifically for ambulatory settings — it delivers image-free intraoperative planning and robotic guidance without cutting blocks, and has a significantly smaller physical footprint than conventional cart-based robotic platforms. Stryker conducted its first cases with the Mako RPS in January 2026, launched a limited market release in February, and plans full commercial rollout in the second half of 2026.
Why It Matters for Michigan MSK The Mako RPS removes two historically prohibitive barriers to robotic TKA adoption in ASCs — physical space and imaging infrastructure. Michigan orthopedic ASCs should assess platform fit now, ahead of full commercial launch, to avoid capacity and credentialing delays when cases ramp.
  • Contact Stryker's regional representative to request a Mako RPS evaluation opportunity during the limited market release and position your ASC for priority placement in the H2 2026 full launch.
  • Commission a total cost of ownership analysis comparing Mako RPS to your current TKA workflow, factoring in OR space, capital cost, training burden, and Michigan ASC reimbursement levels.
  • Monitor published clinical outcomes and surgeon testimonials from early-adopter ASC sites over the next 90 days to build an evidence-based adoption brief for physician and board review.

First Post-FDA-Approval Synergy Disc Procedure Performed at Beverly Hills ASC — Three-Level Cervical Arthroplasty
Surgeons Todd Lanman, MD, and Joel Beckett, MD, performed the first post-FDA-approval Synergy Disc case at Advanced Surgery Center of Beverly Hills on April 8, 2026 — five weeks after the device received FDA premarket approval for single-level cervical disc replacement (C3–C7). The procedure involved a three-level cervical disc replacement. The Synergy Disc features a built-in 6-degree lordotic core designed to preserve spinal motion and improve sagittal alignment, and it demonstrated superiority over ACDF on its U.S. IDE study primary composite endpoint at 24 months.
Why It Matters for Michigan MSK Rapid first-case translation to an ASC setting underscores the accelerating migration of complex cervical spine procedures to outpatient environments. Michigan spine-forward ASCs and practices should monitor newly approved disc replacement devices as competitive differentiators in cervical arthroplasty offerings.
  • Engage your spine surgeons in a quarterly new device review to evaluate the Synergy Disc and other recently cleared cervical arthroplasty options relative to your current device contract portfolio.
  • Track FDA PMA and 510(k) clearance activity for cervical and lumbar disc replacement devices and map coverage and reimbursement timing for your Michigan payer mix.
  • Monitor real-world outcomes data from early-adopter sites as payer and CMS coverage decisions begin to form for the Synergy Disc indication.

Cemented and Cementless TKA with Patella Resurfacing Show Equivalent Clinical Outcomes
Research presented at the AAOS 2026 Annual Meeting found that cemented and cementless total knee arthroplasty with patella resurfacing yielded statistically similar revision rates, reoperation rates, and patient-reported outcome measures. The findings indicate fixation type alone does not determine outcomes in patella resurfacing cases, and that surgeon preference and patient-specific factors can appropriately guide implant selection.
Why It Matters for Michigan MSK Equivalent outcomes across fixation methods provide Michigan orthopedic surgeons and ASC administrators greater flexibility in implant procurement and contract negotiations — enabling cost optimization and portfolio standardization without compromising TKA quality benchmarks.
  • Share this evidence with orthopedic surgeons to inform implant standardization discussions and identify contract consolidation or preferred pricing opportunities with device vendors.
  • Review your TKA implant contracts for opportunities to leverage cemented-cementless equivalence in cost negotiations without compromising surgeon preference or patient outcomes.
  • Incorporate comparative fixation outcome data into patient education and shared decision-making materials for TKA candidates, particularly those with higher revision-risk profiles.

Study: Elevated BMI Does Not Increase Complication Risk in MPFL Reconstruction
Research presented at the AAOS 2026 Annual Meeting found that elevated BMI was not associated with increased complication risk in patients undergoing medial patellofemoral ligament (MPFL) reconstruction, challenging widespread assumptions that obesity meaningfully amplifies risk for this procedure. Authors noted that BMI is an imperfect proxy — it does not capture body composition, musculoskeletal strength, or functional status, which are the factors most directly relevant to surgical outcomes.
Why It Matters for Michigan MSK These findings support broadening MPFL reconstruction candidacy for Michigan MSK patients with elevated BMI, potentially expanding the eligible patient population at practices serving regions with above-average obesity prevalence and improving care equity for patients currently excluded by BMI-based screening thresholds.
  • Audit MPFL reconstruction screening protocols to assess whether BMI-based soft exclusion criteria remain evidence-based in light of this and related emerging research.
  • Brief orthopedic surgeons on the growing evidence base challenging BMI-driven outcome assumptions across MSK procedures to support protocol updates grounded in current literature.
  • Strengthen clinical documentation to capture functional status, body composition, and musculoskeletal strength data beyond BMI to build more robust preauthorization evidence packages for payers.
